❓ Why do we need vectors?

Imagine I tell you to walk 5 metres, have I told you where to go?
Not quite.
A distance isn't enough on its own, we need to know what direction to walk in. Maths uses vectors to solve this problem

📏 What is a vector?

Vectors are mathematical objects which describe both magnitude and direction, unlike an ordinary number, which only says 'how much', a vector also says 'which way'.
An example of a vector is 3m East, where East is the direction and 3m is the magnitude, these simple values and direction are the basis for many complex computer graphics and physics models.

➡️ Why are vectors drawn as arrows?

Arrows naturally represent the two pieces of information a vector contains, their length shows magnitude, and their arrowhead showing direction. This makes them a good way to visualise movement, forces, and positions.

They are typically represented by an arrow where length represents direction and arrowhead shows direction, the starting point is called the tail and the endpoint is called the head.

📍 Component form and coordinates

Although arrows are useful for visualising vectors, computers cannot store arrows. Instead they store vectors as numbers describing how far something moves along each axis. For example: (3,2) means move 3 units right and then 2 upwards.

Algebraically, vectors are shows in component form, such as v→=⟨vx,vy,vz⟩ in a three-dimensional space, where each compentent describes how far the vector points along one of the coordinate axis

➕ How does vector addition work?

Vector addition is most simply explained when drawn graphically, by arranging the vectors so that their heads touch the tail of the next vector. Draw a resultant vector by drawing a line from the first vectors tail to the head of the last vector. The resultant magnitude is the length of the line, and the direction is the angle between the line and the x-axis.

Alternatively, Pythagorean Theorem and trigonometry can be used to give more accurate magnitudes and directions, however further calculations must be undergone to calculate the accurate angle for direction.

This works as movement can be broken into separate steps. Walking 3m east and then 4m north ends at exactly the same place as walking along the single resultant vector.

This often is used in free body diagram in physics to determine the magnitude and direction of forces on objects.

🎮 Vectors in computer graphics

In Computer Graphics, vectors are used:

  • To locate objects
  • To move objects
  • To rotate objects
  • To scale objects
  • To calculate lighting
  • To calculate surface normals
  • To calculate camera direction
  • To complete animations
  • To make particle systems

Vectors make spatial relationships precise and efficient, operations such as addition and subtractions, dot product, and cross product. These allow for visuals to be realistic and quick to produce.

They are the cornerstone of computer graphics and allow for the representation and manipulation of objects in a digital space.

⚡ Vectors in physics

Vectors can be added or subtracted as seen before, but scalar multiplication (multiplying a number by a vector) allows a vectors magnitude to be changed without changing its direction.

In Physics, vectors can be used for mechanics, electromagnetism, quantum mechanics and engineering. In mechanics, vectors describe the displacement, velocity, acceleration and forces that are acting on an object. This allows for physicists to determine different things that are acting on the object.

In electromagnetism, electric and magnetic fields are vector fields so each point in that space has a vector indicating the fields magnitude and direction, so vectors are crucial for calculating forces in the field

In quantum mechanics, states of particles are represented by vectors, which although being abstract, follow vector space rules to describe probabilites and observables.

In engineering, vectors are used to analyse force in structures, control robotic movements, and stimulate computer graphics and animations.

📜 A brief history of vectors

The word 'vector' originates from the Latin vehere, meaning 'to carry' and was used in astronomical geometry in the 17th and 19th centuries to describe lines representing motion.

Vectors were used before they had a name, early mathematicians and scientists used directed lines to represent forces and velocities, although they didn't formalise the notion of a vector. In the late 18th and 19th centuries, a complex number was interpreted geometrically as points on a plane, essentially being a two-dimensional vector.

In 1827, August Ferdinand Möbius used 'vectors' in his work The Barycentric Calculus, defining operations such as addition and scalar multiplication, beginning to start how vector arithmetic would work, though initially it was overlooked.

William Rowan Hamilton, in the 1830s and 1840s sough to extend complex numbers into three dimensions, his solution was quaternions in 1843. They are expressed as a + bi + cj + dk, where i, j, k are orthogonal units. Hamilton distinguished the scalar point a from the vector part bi + cj + dk, beginning to provide the framework for 3D vectors.

In the late 1800s, Gibbs and Heaviside simplified vectors into the notation we still use today.
